Nordic Semiconductor: Bridging the "Empty State" Trust Gap

Format

72-Hour Interview Take-Home

Company

Nordic Semiconductor

Focus

Scooter-fleet management platform

Problem Statement

New customers stared at an empty dashboard for days while technicians provisioned hardware, and many abandoned before ever seeing value.

What I Did

Scoped the problem down to four investigative questions, benchmarked comparable products, and designed a transparency-first provisioning flow.

Fig: Provisioning status tracker replacing the empty dashboard state

The Problem

Hardware-dependent provisioning created a multi-day trust gap.

New users signed up expecting immediate value, but their dashboard stayed empty until a technician physically provisioned their scooters — sometimes days later. Without any signal that progress was happening, users lost confidence in the product before it had a chance to prove itself.

Technician timelines

What should users realistically expect, and when should that expectation be communicated?

Duplicate prevention

How does the system avoid double-provisioning the same device or account?

Role-based notifications

Who needs to know what, and at which step of setup?

Insurance approval

Where does the approval workflow sit relative to provisioning and go-live?

Research & Benchmarking

Other products already solved pieces of this problem.

I benchmarked against Joyride and ScootAPI for fleet-management patterns, then borrowed further from outside the category: Stripe's test mode and AWS's pre-populated demo environments, both of which let users explore a fully-functioning product before any real data exists.

Solution

Make the wait itself the product experience.

The core solution was a Provisioning Status Tracker: instead of a blank dashboard, users see exactly which technical setup steps remain, turning an anxious wait into a transparent, trackable process.

Revenue Dashboard & MVP

The dashboard had to answer three business questions, not display every metric.

Once live, the dashboard needed to answer: is the fleet profitable, how well are scooters being utilized, and where can operational costs come down? I recommended shipping only the core fleet-management view with basic financial metrics as the MVP, rather than a fully-loaded reporting suite.

On the engineering side, I scoped for Mapbox with stale-while-revalidate caching, clustering to support 10k+ devices, and prioritizing critical alerts over full fleet visibility when the map gets dense.

User Psychology & Trade-offs

Mock fleets built ownership before real data existed.

I used the endowment effect deliberately: showing a mock fleet during setup builds a sense of ownership and momentum, so I designed the transition from simulated to real data to soften the emotional dip when the mock scooters disappear.

Within the 72-hour constraint, I prioritized getting the state-transition logic right over feature breadth, and trust-building over comprehensive reporting.

Testing Strategy

Time-to-value is the metric that matters here.

I proposed measuring time-to-value improvements directly against the old empty-state experience, plus load-testing geospatial performance at enterprise fleet scale to confirm the clustering approach holds up beyond the take-home's scope.
