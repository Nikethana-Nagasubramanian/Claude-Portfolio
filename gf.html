Context

Retail expansion is a high-stakes decision, but the workflow was scattered.

Analysts were piecing together maps, spreadsheets, broker lists, demographics, competitors, traffic, and internal assumptions.

The goal was not just a better interface; it was to become the first place teams go when evaluating a location.

My Role

I owned the product shape from raw signals to shipped workflow.

As founding designer, I led strategy, research, information architecture, visual design, and early product direction.

I worked closely with founders, real estate stakeholders, and users to turn fragmented data into a repeatable decision system.

Core Shift

I started by thinking users needed better access to data.

Research showed the real need was a decision framework: what signals matter, how they relate, and when to surface them.

Discovery

I simulated the workflow before designing the product.

Without early user access, I evaluated Boston locations as if I were choosing a site for a taco truck.

That gave me a baseline for the pressure, ambiguity, and cost behind every location decision.

Fig: User journey and findings from user interviews

Design Principles

The map should do 75% of the storytelling.

The data should support the remaining 25%, giving analysts confidence without forcing them to parse every raw signal.

This principle helped me simplify the interface while preserving the depth real estate teams needed.

Final Solution

The final workflow turned scattered inputs into one evaluation system.

Analysts could search an address, review market signals, understand competitors and complements, and decide whether a site deserved deeper evaluation.

Early versions prioritized data completeness; later versions made the map the hero because that is where users found meaning fastest.

Decision Framework

The product organized evaluation around market, site, brand, and demographic signals.

This created a foundation for future ranking and comparison workflows without overwhelming non-technical users.

We balanced structured inputs with user-friendly language so the system could evolve toward smarter recommendations later.

Visual slot: decision framework, signal hierarchy, or before-and-after IA.

Reflection

This project taught me how to design for trust in data-heavy workflows.

Users need speed, but they also need to understand why a recommendation makes sense.

My strongest contribution was turning fragmented signals into a product system people could repeatedly act on.
